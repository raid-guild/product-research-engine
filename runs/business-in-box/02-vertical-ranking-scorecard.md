# Vertical Ranking Scorecard

Scoring uses a 100-point practical attractiveness score for a founder-led SaaS wedge. Higher is better. Criteria include pain urgency, willingness to pay, reachability, low software sophistication, workflow frequency, repeat customer potential, manageable regulatory complexity, manageable competitive intensity, and founder-led sales difficulty.

| Rank | Vertical | Score | Rationale |
|---:|---|---:|---|
| 1 | Handyman / home repair | 82 | Large fragmented market, recurring admin pain, moderate ability to pay, lower regulation than licensed trades, less incumbent lock-in than HVAC/plumbing. |
| 2 | Residential cleaning | 76 | High repeat potential and simple workflows; lower willingness to pay and more price pressure. Good backup for a lighter recurring-ops package. |
| 3 | Landscaping / lawn care | 73 | Large market and recurring routes; seasonal complexity and existing route/field-service tools create sharper product requirements. |
| 4 | Pressure washing / exterior cleaning | 69 | Easy to reach and low regulation; smaller market, seasonal demand, and many side-hustle buyers may limit ARPA. |
| 5 | Mobile mechanics | 67 | Urgent jobs and customer communication pain; more operational complexity, trust issues, and marketplace competition. |
| 6 | Food trucks | 64 | Clear operational pain but POS, permitting, location, inventory, and health department complexity make the product less aligned with the proposed records/admin wedge. |
| 7 | Plumbing | 62 | Very high willingness to pay and urgent jobs, but heavier licensing, stronger incumbents, higher expectations, and harder sales. |
| 8 | HVAC | 60 | High-ticket vertical with strong software spend; competitive field-service category is mature and buyer expectations are high. |
| 9 | Electrical | 58 | Large market and strong revenue, but licensing/regulatory concerns and job complexity make this harder for a first wedge. |

## Recommended Starting Vertical

Start with handyman/home repair.

Target the operator who is past the "side hustle" stage but has not operationalized the business:

- One owner, sometimes one helper.
- Uses text messages, phone photos, paper notes, Venmo/Zelle/Square, spreadsheets, or a basic invoice app.
- Gets enough inbound demand that admin leakage costs money.
- Is not ready for a heavy field-service suite.

## Top 3 Vertical Notes

### 1. Handyman / Home Repair

Must solve:

- Inquiry capture from texts/calls/forms.
- Estimate templates.
- Job scheduling and reminders.
- Before/after photos and job notes.
- Invoice and payment follow-up.
- Monthly receipt and income packet for accountant.

Why it wins:

- IBISWorld reports about 529k U.S. handyman services businesses in 2026.
- Workflows are frequent but not as technically specialized as HVAC/plumbing.
- Many operators sell trust and responsiveness, so follow-up and review automation directly affect revenue.

Main risks:

- Licensing rules vary by state and job type.
- Some operators are too small or casual to pay $149/month.
- Boundary needed around "what work can I legally perform?"

### 2. Cleaning

Must solve:

- Recurring schedule.
- Client instructions.
- No-show/late cancellation policy.
- Payment collection.
- Review/referral prompts.
- Simple payroll/contractor handoff later.

Why it is attractive:

- IBISWorld reports more than 1.25 million U.S. janitorial services businesses in 2026.
- Repeat cadence is strong.
- Lower compliance complexity than licensed trades.

Main risks:

- Lower ARPA.
- Many operators are extremely price-sensitive.
- Residential and commercial cleaning have different workflows.

### 3. Landscaping / Lawn Care

Must solve:

- Recurring route scheduling.
- Seasonal reactivation.
- Quotes for cleanups and enhancements.
- Crew notes/photos.
- Invoice batching.
- Review and referral loops.

Why it is attractive:

- IBISWorld reports about 556k U.S. landscaping services businesses in 2026 and a $176.7B 2026 market size.
- Repeat services create retention potential.
- Operators often feel seasonal cash-flow pressure.

Main risks:

- Route optimization and crew dispatch expectations can pull the product into deeper field-service territory.
- Seasonality complicates revenue and churn.

## Rationale For Avoiding Weaker Initial Verticals

Plumbing, HVAC, and electrical are attractive but are not the best first wedge. They have high willingness to pay, but incumbent software is stronger, regulatory expectations are higher, and the buyer may expect dispatch, price books, financing, inventory, warranties, service agreements, and technician management.

Food trucks need POS, inventory, menus, permits, commissary workflows, event scheduling, and health compliance. That is a different product.

Pressure washing is useful as a later adjacent test because it resembles handyman workflows, but the market is smaller and more seasonal.
