# Business-in-a-Box Research Plan

## Research Objective

Validate whether a U.S.-focused SaaS product, powered by an AI copilot behind the scenes, can help very small businesses get operational, stay organized, and prepare clean business records without offering legal or tax advice.

The core hypothesis:

> Small service businesses do not want more software. They want to be operational, paid, organized, and ready for accounting or tax handoff without learning QuickBooks, Wix, Stripe, Mailchimp, Calendly, and CRM software separately.

## Current Assumptions

- Geography: United States only.
- Initial vertical: undecided.
- Business model: SaaS with an AI copilot behind the scenes.
- Customer type: both brand-new businesses and existing operators.
- Constraint: no legal or tax advice.
- Allowed tax-adjacent value: organize records, produce summaries, export reports, and prepare accountant-ready packets.
- Founder strengths: software/product engineering.
- Founder weaknesses: marketing, GTM, and sales.
- Desired outcome: cash-flow business is acceptable; venture-scale is not required.
- Early concierge onboarding is acceptable for validation.
- Founder-led sales calls are acceptable.
- Research depth: standard memo.
- Initial cash-flow planning target: $10,000/month in recurring revenue, used as a baseline checkpoint rather than an upper limit.
- Support preference: automate as much as possible and avoid support-heavy customer segments when possible.
- Partnership openness: willing to partner with bookkeepers, accountants, and formation providers.

## Positioning Hypotheses

### Position A: Business-in-a-Box

Promise:

> Start a legitimate business in a weekend.

Research question:

Is business formation painful enough, frequent enough, and monetizable enough to support a recurring SaaS business after setup is complete?

### Position B: Digital Operations Department

Promise:

> We handle the boring business stuff so you can do the work.

Research question:

Do existing small service businesses have enough recurring operational pain around invoices, jobs, payments, customer follow-up, bookkeeping records, and reviews to pay monthly?

### Position C: Modern Successor to QuickBooks

Promise:

> Connects everything and manages it for you.

Research question:

Can the product grow from a narrow operations wedge into a broader small-business operating system?

## Recommended Starting Hypothesis

Start research with Position B: Digital Operations Department.

Reason:

Existing operators already have customers, revenue, operational mess, and a clearer ability to pay. Brand-new business formation can remain an expansion path, but it may be harder to monetize as the first wedge because setup is episodic.

## Research Workstreams

### 1. Vertical Selection

Compare likely U.S. service-business wedges:

- handyman
- cleaning
- landscaping
- pressure washing
- HVAC
- plumbing
- electrical
- mobile mechanics
- food trucks
- small local service operators

Score each vertical by:

- pain urgency
- willingness to pay
- ease of reaching buyers
- software sophistication
- workflow frequency
- repeat customer potential
- regulatory complexity
- competitive intensity
- founder-led sales difficulty

Output:

- ranked top 3 verticals
- recommended starting vertical
- rationale for avoiding weaker verticals

### 2. Customer Pain and Workflow Mapping

Map the actual operating workflow for the top verticals:

- customer inquiry
- quote or estimate
- scheduling
- job tracking
- invoicing
- payment collection
- customer follow-up
- review request
- repeat booking
- bookkeeping export
- tax-prep record organization

Research questions:

- Where do operators lose time?
- Where do they lose money?
- Where do they currently use spreadsheets, texts, paper, or memory?
- Which pains are annoying versus urgent?
- Which pains create willingness to pay?

Output:

- workflow map
- pain-point ranking
- "must solve" versus "nice to have" list

### 3. Competitive Landscape

Analyze competitors and substitutes across categories:

- formation: LegalZoom, ZenBusiness, Tailor Brands
- accounting: QuickBooks, Wave
- payments: Square, Stripe
- websites: Wix, Squarespace
- local service SaaS: Jobber, Housecall Pro
- CRM and client workflows: HoneyBook
- payroll and back office: Gusto
- banking and finance: Novo, Mercury
- DIY alternatives: spreadsheets, texts, notes, paper, accountants, bookkeepers

Evaluate:

- pricing
- target customer
- onboarding friction
- feature scope
- vertical focus
- AI positioning
- integrations
- customer complaints
- switching barriers

Output:

- competitive map
- pricing table
- positioning gaps
- differentiation opportunities

### 4. Pricing and Packaging

Research likely price bands:

- starter: $29/month
- solo operator: $79-$149/month
- operations-heavy business: $199-$399/month
- setup-assisted: one-time setup fee plus monthly subscription

Use the $10,000/month cash-flow planning target as a baseline pricing sanity check. This is not the ceiling; stronger upside is preferred.

- at $49/month: about 205 customers
- at $99/month: about 102 customers
- at $149/month: about 68 customers
- at $249/month: about 41 customers
- at $399/month: about 26 customers

Research questions:

- What do target customers already pay for operations software?
- Do they prefer monthly SaaS, setup fee, or bundled service?
- Is "AI copilot" valuable language, or should it stay invisible?
- Which outcome creates the strongest willingness to pay: getting paid, saving time, reducing chaos, or getting more jobs?
- Which pricing model reaches at least $10,000/month with the least support burden and leaves room for meaningful upside?

Output:

- pricing hypothesis
- packaging options
- recommended first offer
- customer count required to reach $10,000/month and larger upside scenarios

### 5. Customer Validation Design

Interview both:

- brand-new business owners
- existing small service-business operators

Test messages:

- "Start your business in a weekend."
- "Your digital operations department."
- "Invoices, payments, customers, reminders, and records in one place."
- "Get accountant-ready reports without learning bookkeeping software."
- "We handle the admin so you can do the work."

Validation goals:

- identify strongest buyer segment
- identify top pain points
- test willingness to pay
- discover current alternatives
- validate buying triggers
- learn channel behavior

Output:

- interview script
- respondent screener
- validation scorecard
- synthesis template

### 6. Legal and Tax Boundary

Define safe product boundaries.

The product can:

- provide business setup checklists
- organize documents
- track income and expenses
- categorize transactions based on user-approved rules
- generate reports
- export CPA-ready packets
- remind users about dates and missing records
- route users to professionals or official sources

The product should not:

- recommend a legal entity as legal advice
- provide tax strategy as tax advice
- file legal or tax documents without a qualified partner flow
- represent itself as a lawyer, accountant, or tax preparer

Output:

- compliance boundary memo
- product language guidelines
- risky feature list

### 7. MVP Definition

Evaluate three possible MVPs.

#### MVP A: Start Kit

Helps a new business get operational:

- business setup checklist
- domain and website setup
- business email
- payment link
- invoice template
- basic records folder
- launch task list

#### MVP B: Ops Kit

Helps an existing business stay organized:

- customer database
- quote or invoice workflow
- payment tracking
- job reminders
- review request workflow
- records dashboard
- bookkeeping export

#### MVP C: Growth Kit

Helps an operating business get more customers:

- Google Business Profile tasks
- review campaigns
- follow-up messages
- referral prompts
- simple email or SMS campaigns

Recommended research bias:

Prioritize MVP B first. Existing operators have active pain and clearer monthly willingness to pay.

Output:

- MVP recommendation
- feature scope
- no-build concierge test
- first 30-day build plan

### 8. Go-to-Market Research

Because the founder team is strong in software but weak in marketing, GTM risk must be treated as a first-class research area.

Research channels:

- local Facebook groups
- trade communities
- Google search intent
- YouTube how-to content
- accountant and bookkeeper partnerships
- small business formation content
- cold outreach to newly registered businesses
- local chambers of commerce
- vertical-specific forums and communities

Research questions:

- Where do these buyers already ask for help?
- What language do they use for the problem?
- Which channels allow cheap validation?
- Is founder-led sales required?
- Are partners a better wedge than direct acquisition?
- Which channels can reach at least $10,000/month without requiring paid ads as the primary engine?
- Which partner channels reduce trust friction without creating heavy services work?

Output:

- first 3 channel tests
- outreach scripts
- landing page messaging angles
- GTM risk assessment
- partner-channel shortlist

## Success Criteria

The idea should continue if research shows:

- a specific vertical has urgent recurring operations pain
- customers already pay for inferior or fragmented solutions
- the first product can be built without regulated advice
- a monthly price of at least $79-$149 is plausible
- a believable path to at least $10,000/month exists without hundreds of high-support accounts
- the first acquisition channel can be tested without large ad spend
- the MVP can create an obvious outcome within the first week of use
- concierge onboarding can validate demand without becoming the permanent product

The idea should narrow or pivot if:

- the market only wants one-time setup help
- existing vertical SaaS already solves the urgent workflows well
- customers need too much handholding for SaaS economics
- legal or tax expectations become central to the promise
- no reachable customer segment shows willingness to pay
- even reaching the initial $10,000/month checkpoint requires a customer count or support load that does not fit the founder constraints
