# Market Opportunity Memo

## Executive Summary

Recommendation: continue, but narrow the initial wedge to an operations product for existing solo and small-crew handyman/home-repair operators.

The broad "business-in-a-box" idea is attractive but too diffuse as a first product. Formation is episodic, crowded, and full of legal/tax expectation risk. The stronger wedge is Position B: a digital operations department for operators who already have customers, revenue, and administrative leakage.

The first offer should not be sold as "AI." The buyer does not want an AI tool. They want fewer missed jobs, faster invoices, cleaner records, automatic follow-up, and less weekend admin. AI should power setup, categorization, reminders, message drafting, and exception handling behind the scenes.

## Core Thesis

Very small service businesses already pay for fragments of the desired bundle: Jobber/Housecall Pro for field service operations, QuickBooks/Wave for books, Square/Stripe for payments, Wix/Squarespace for web presence, HoneyBook for client workflows, Gusto for payroll, and formation providers for setup. The opportunity is not to replace every system on day one. It is to package the messy middle into a narrow, low-learning workflow:

> "Get your jobs, invoices, payments, follow-ups, and accountant-ready records under control this week."

## Market Signals

- The U.S. has more than 36.2 million small businesses, employing 62.3 million people and representing 99.9 percent of U.S. businesses, according to SBA Office of Advocacy 2026 FAQ data.
- Business creation remains high. The Census Bureau reported 523,971 seasonally adjusted business applications in May 2026, up 3.7 percent from April 2026.
- The best relevant vertical pools are large enough for a cash-flow SaaS: IBISWorld public snapshots list about 528,883 U.S. handyman services businesses in 2026, 556,238 landscaping services businesses in 2026, 1,254,202 janitorial services businesses in 2026, 128,787 plumbing businesses in 2026, 120,461 HVAC contractors in 2026, and 261,958 electrician businesses in 2026.
- Competitor pricing supports a $99-$249/month band for small operators. Jobber lists Core at $49/month monthly or $29/month annually, with Connect/Grow tiers climbing into $139-$399 monthly list pricing. Housecall Pro positions itself for 200,000 pros across 50+ trades and starts from $59/month. QuickBooks Online list pricing ranges from $38/month to $275/month before promotions. HoneyBook annual plans run $29, $49, and $109/month.

## Best Initial Segment

Primary ICP: U.S. solo or 1-3 person handyman/home-repair operator doing $8k-$50k/month in revenue, receiving jobs from referrals, Google Business Profile, local Facebook groups, property managers, and repeat homeowners.

Why this segment:

- Frequent workflow: estimates, scheduling, job notes, invoices, payment collection, follow-up, receipts.
- Enough pain to buy: missed follow-ups, unpaid invoices, tax-season scramble, scattered texts/photos/receipts.
- Lower regulatory burden than HVAC, plumbing, or electrical, though state/local licensing still matters.
- Less saturated by enterprise field-service software than HVAC/plumbing.
- Easier founder-led discovery through local groups, marketplace posts, property manager referrals, and small business networks.

## Why Not Start With Formation

Formation products are crowded and episodic. LegalZoom starts LLC formation at $0 plus state fees; ZenBusiness and Tailor Brands also offer $0 plus state fees entry packages, with higher annual bundles. These companies already monetize formation through upsells, compliance, registered agent services, banking, websites, and partner offers.

A new product can include startup checklists and document organization later, but leading with "start a legitimate business in a weekend" risks:

- One-time purchase behavior.
- Unclear recurring value after setup.
- Legal/tax-advice expectations.
- High competition from formation incumbents with paid acquisition engines and affiliate programs.

## Product Positioning

Lead with:

> "Your admin back office for handyman jobs."

Supporting promise:

> "Quotes, invoices, payment reminders, review requests, job notes, receipts, and accountant-ready monthly packets, set up for you."

Avoid leading with:

- "AI copilot"
- "QuickBooks replacement"
- "Legal business setup"
- "Tax optimization"

## First Offer

Recommended first offer:

- $499 one-time Admin Recovery setup.
- $149/month Solo Ops subscription.
- $249/month Small Crew subscription for up to 3 field users.
- Optional $399/month Concierge Ops pilot only for the first 10 validation customers, with a hard cap on manual work.

The $10k/month checkpoint requires about 67 customers at $149/month or 40 customers at $249/month. A blended base of 35 Solo Ops customers and 20 Small Crew customers reaches about $10,195 MRR before setup fees.

## Strategic Risks

- Vertical SaaS incumbents already solve many workflows for buyers ready to adopt software.
- Low-tech operators may require high onboarding support.
- Price-sensitive segments may treat this as "just invoicing."
- The product must avoid drifting into tax preparation, legal entity advice, or bookkeeping services that require professional oversight.
- GTM is the largest risk because founder strengths are product/engineering rather than marketing/sales.

## Decision

Continue, but narrow.

Build and validate MVP B: Ops Kit for handyman/home-repair operators. Run a concierge onboarding test before building broad integrations. Formation should remain an expansion layer, not the first wedge.
