## Research Workstreams

### 1. Segment Selection

Compare likely customer/user wedges.

Score each by:

- pain urgency
- willingness to pay or adopt
- ease of reaching users
- workflow frequency
- switching friction
- support burden
- regulatory or trust complexity
- competitive intensity
- founder-led validation difficulty

Output:

- ranked top 3 segments
- recommended starting segment
- rationale for avoiding weaker segments

### 2. Customer Pain And Workflow Mapping

Map the current workflow and pain points.

Output:

- workflow map
- pain ranking
- must-solve vs nice-to-have list
- willingness-to-pay or willingness-to-adopt triggers

### 3. Competitive Landscape

Analyze direct competitors, indirect competitors, and substitutes.

Output:

- competitive map
- pricing or monetization table
- positioning gaps
- differentiation opportunities

### 4. Pricing, Packaging, Or Adoption Model

Research likely adoption and monetization paths.

Output:

- pricing or adoption hypothesis
- packaging options
- recommended first offer
- path to first meaningful revenue or usage milestone

### 5. Customer Validation Design

Design interviews, reaction tests, or landing page/message tests.

Output:

- interview script
- respondent screener
- validation scorecard
- synthesis template

### 6. Risk Boundary

Define legal, trust, operational, support, and brand boundaries.

Output:

- risk boundary memo
- language guidelines
- risky feature list

### 7. MVP Definition

Evaluate likely MVP shapes and no-build tests.

Output:

- MVP recommendation
- feature scope
- explicit non-scope
- no-build or concierge test
- first 30-day plan

### 8. Go-To-Market Research

Treat distribution risk as a first-class research area.

Output:

- first 3 channel tests
- outreach scripts
- landing page messaging angles
- GTM risk assessment
- partner-channel shortlist

## Success Criteria

The idea should continue if research shows:

- a specific segment has urgent pain or strong desire
- reachable users already spend time, money, attention, or social capital on alternatives
- the first product can deliver an obvious outcome quickly
- a plausible acquisition path exists without heroic paid spend
- the support or trust burden fits the founder constraints
- a narrow MVP can be tested before building the full vision

The idea should narrow, pivot, or pause if:

- the buyer or user remains too broad
- the strongest demand is for a different product shape
- existing alternatives already solve the urgent part well
- distribution appears harder than the value of the wedge
- the product depends on risky claims or regulated behavior
